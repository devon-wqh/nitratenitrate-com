A Forray into Directing Through the Mundane Topic of Arachnophilia

Interview and article by Tori Frank

Spider, a short film by Cate Christiansen starring Leandro McPhee, is about a guy who jerks off to spiders. Obviously. When I met Cate at Mora cafe in Williamsburg to talk about the film, I was buzzing off of a latte on a rainy day in May. I had questions, and she had answers.

T: You're the writer, director, co-producer and editor of Spider. Do you feel most at home in any of these roles as a filmmaker?

C: The first role I ever did in film in general is editing– I edited dance films for a girl I know who was getting her masters in dance. That was kind of my entry point into film, and then I fell into producing. My roommate at the time was a DP, and she would be working on all these different projects, and she needed more help making the film happen, which is not really the DP's job. So she would pitch me as a producer for the director. And then things just sort of snowballed. By the time I graduated from NYU, I had produced probably a dozen films.

And so when I was making Spider, I felt most at home in producing. Spider was the first film I ever directed.

T: Wow, congrats. I would never have guessed that. What was it like moving into the writer / director role with Spider?

C: I wrote the script while I didn't feel like paying attention in class. It's based on a story someone had told me about some guy. I was really connected with the story, and then because I have producer brain, I just approached it with the mindset of how do I make this happen, and what can I actually make happen for a camera? It's like all about creating a context, and producing is finding all of the parts that go into how to produce that context: where is this taking place, what people are there to capture it, what people are on screen, and finding those resources to make them all come together at the same time.

T: I would listen to a masterclass of you explaining this process. Do you want to direct more now?

C: Yes, I'm working on my next film right now. I'm shooting in September, and I have more producers this time, which is great. But yeah, I definitely want to continue directing. I think it was really easy to direct Spider.

We did a lot of rehearsals before we shot with the actors – probably like ten rehearsals – where we would just be in an empty room and stage it like a play, and then I would stand around in different parts of the room as though my eyes are the camera, and then I built the short list from there.

That's what I mean by creating contexts.

T: Do you like to draw out the shots?

C: I just remember. I have a very photographic memory.

T: Did you rehearse on location as well?

C: We did a scout in the house that we shot it in about a month and a half before we shot, so when I came back to the rehearsals I knew the room, how big it is, this is where the table is, this is where I'm thinking the camera will go, etc., etc.

The scout was me and the DP (Lex / Alexandra Mettler). I'd go back in the rehearsals with the actors, and think what is important in this scene? What do I want to see in this scene? And then Lex made it look pretty.

T: Speaking of looking good, the film is gorgeous. Was shooting on film an important point for you?

C: Yeah. The first film I ever produced was shot on film, and I just really liked working with film and having a limited amount of material.

But obviously for that project, I was not directing or involved with the camera at all. So when it came to my own project, it's basically just like a big math problem. Each roll of film has 11 minutes of footage, so how many takes can you get? Building a shot list that works with film is basically just a game of how little coverage we can get.

I really like it. When I was 16, I did a semester abroad for high school students in Paris, and I did French immersion and minored in 35 millimeter photography because I thought it would be a fun minor.

But then I got really into celluloid photography. So I feel like the process of switching from stills to motion pictures was pretty sensical to me. You know, there's only 24 to 36 shots per roll. So the way you space that out in your head is kind of the same as how you space out a roll of film.

So much of that film was just like magic to me.

T: How premeditated was the visual style?

C: So much of that film was just like magic to me.

I give so much credit to Lex, the DP, and her relationship with the gaffer Liam Podos. They work really, really well together. So much of what they did is like science. They use lighting science to make it look a certain way. And then everything really, really came together with the colorist Nick Daukas. It's a very brown film, and it was not originally colored like that. He colored it to have that feeling.

T: Many people have said the look and performances of Spider remind them of Yorgos Lanthimos. What's your reaction to that comparison?

C: Killing of a Sacred Deer was in our original pitch deck as one of our references.

I think the crossover would be that he's obviously not a neurotypical character. And I think that's most of what Yorgos Lanthimos films are about. I think the main difference between him and me is that he leans more heavily into the othering of that character versus I was leaning into really trying to humanize and empathize with this weirdo.

Things used to be in completely different places in order. And part of why I chose to change the order around was it felt like I was withholding the reveal that this is a kid who jerks off to spiders, and so instead of withholding that information, I was like, I'm just going to put it straight at the beginning. But I didn't want it to use shock value.

T: I do find myself curious about his proclivity more so than disturbed. I have to ask, is the reveal at the end a spider humanoid baby?

C: Yeah. It is. He gives his cum to the spiders, and then there's a spider humanoid baby.

It intercuts from him at the dinner table to the reveal, then back to the dinner table. I was trying to leave it open as to whether or not that actually happened or if that's his fantasy. I like believing that it really happens.

T: I also like how that adds to the fantasy. Not just getting off from the spider itself, but the idea of creation (or recreation).

C: I remember bringing that version of the script to the lead actor Leandro. I visited him at work, and I was like, I have a new draft for you! It's fucking crazy.

That's why I had an intimacy coordinator. It's not like he even was really touching himself, we weren't really even doing simulated masturbation, he just made voices. But he's also my friend in real life.

T: Does that make doing those scenes easier?

C: It helps to feel comfortable. But I didn't want to feel like because we're friends, I could assume that everything was fine. So I wanted to have an intermediary there to know he's comfortable and he has an advocate just in case. But she ended up not really even doing anything on set. I mean, she did, but he was chill the whole process.

Everyone is a little bit off-put by it, which I've learned most festivals don't like, which is fine.

T: Spider has played at the Reykjavik International Film Festival in Iceland, the New/Next Film Festival, and the Downtown Festival. How have people been reacting to it so far as you've shown it around?

C: It's been a really amazing thing for my ego to accept that everybody receives the film differently. And like, that's the most fun part. Some people see it as a comedy, some people see it as really sad. It's fun to hear the reactions all across the spectrum of like, loved, hated. Nobody's hated it though.

In terms of submitting it to festivals, it's a very "off" film. Regardless of whether or not someone receives it as sad or horror or funny, everyone is a little bit off-put by it, which I've learned most festivals don't like, which is fine.

I have read all of the Letterbox reviews. There's one that stuck in my head that was like, I don't know why everyone in the audience was laughing. I found this deeply sad. I think that summarizes the film pretty well.
